Tell Trenton: Fully Fund the Formula. Stop Scaling Down Our Schools.

New Jersey's school funding formula (SFRA) calculates exactly what each district needs for a thorough and efficient education — the Adequacy Budget — and how much of that a community can reasonably cover locally. The state is supposed to pay the rest through Equalization Aid.

In practice, it doesn't work that way. The state decides how much aid money is available first, then applies a scaling multiplier that shrinks the amount of aid each district receives to fit inside that fixed pot — regardless of what the formula actually says students need. The result is a funding formula that looks fair on paper but quietly delivers less than it promises. Here's what that means for Audubon:

  • Audubon is still underfunded, two years running. Even after raising local taxes, the district was $2.5 million below the state's own adequacy target in 2025-26, and is still projected to be about $1.5 million short in 2026-27.
  • Real costs aren't reimbursed. Audubon's transportation aid covers only a fraction of actual costs — roughly $74,000 in aid against $851,550 in projected costs, a $777,000 gap the district has to cover with general education funds.
  • Year-to-year swings make planning impossible. Arbitrary caps let the state cut aid by up to 3% or raise it by up to 6% each year, unrelated to a district's actual, calculated need.
  • The pressure compounds. In 2026-27 alone, Audubon faces a $205,000 cut in state aid at the same time health care costs are rising 37% (over $858,000) — forcing the district to make difficult decisions which resulted in a 7.59% property tax hike and roughly $725,000 in additional cuts.

The fix is simple:

  1. Fully fund the Adequacy Budget as calculated,
  2. Eliminate the scaling multiplier that shrinks it to fit a fixed appropriation, and
  3. Make the caps on state aid cuts permanent so districts can plan responsibly.

NJ's School Funding Formula

New Jersey's formula calculates what each district actually needs (the Adequacy Budget), then is supposed to cover the gap beyond what a community can locally afford. Instead, the state sets its total aid budget first and applies a scaling multiplier that shrinks every district's aid to fit — regardless of calculated need. For Audubon, that means:

  • Still under-adequacy, two years running: $2.5 million short in 2025-26, and roughly $1.5 million short in 2026-27 — even after local tax increases.
  • Real costs go unreimbursed: a $777,000 gap between actual transportation costs and the state aid meant to cover them.
  • Unpredictable year-to-year swings: state aid can be cut by up to 3% or raised by up to 6% annually, with no relationship to actual district need.
  • Compounding pressure: a $205,000 state aid cut arriving the same year health care costs jump 37%, forcing a 7.59% property tax hike.

We're calling on our District 5 legislators to eliminate the scaling multiplier and fully fund schools based on the formula's own calculated need — not a fixed appropriation.

Who We Are

Audubon Community Advocates is a group of concerned neighbors, parents, and residents of Audubon, NJ who came together because we believe our schools, our educators, and our students deserve better.

We are not a political party or a government body — we're everyday members of this community who care about the future of Audubon's schools and the taxpayers who fund them. We formed to give local residents a direct, simple way to make their voices heard on the issues that affect our schools, starting with New Jersey's broken school funding formula.

Whether you're a parent, an educator, or simply a neighbor who cares about this town, we welcome you to join us.
